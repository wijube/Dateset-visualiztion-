Addressing Teenage Pregnancy: Disparities, Interventions, and Data Insights

Data Insights

Teenage pregnancy and its repercussions extend beyond mere statistics; they have far-reaching implications for both young mothers and their offspring. Adolescent mothers face heightened risks of severe health complications, including conditions such as eclampsia, puerperal endometritis, and systemic infections, as highlighted by the World Health Organization (WHO, 2023). Equally concerning are the challenges faced by their infants, who are predisposed to low birth weight, preterm birth, and severe neonatal conditions (WHO, 2023).

Global figures paint a stark reality: in 2019 alone, a staggering 21 million girls under the age of 20 experienced pregnancy (WHO, 2023). This issue is not uniform across nations, with the highest rates of teen births among western industrialized countries recorded in the United States (Sedgh et al., 2015). Recent data from 2019 revealed approximately 17.4 births per 1000 females aged 15-19 in the USA, indicating a persistent challenge (CDC, 2021).

Interventions

Encouragingly, the implementation of publicly funded family planning clinics offering affordable services for adolescents has played a pivotal role in curbing these rates (Elflein, 2023). As a result, an estimated 900,000 pregnancies, births, and abortions among teens in the United States have potentially been averted (Elflein, 2023). Moreover, there has been a commendable rise in contraceptive use among adolescents, contributing significantly to this downward trend (Elflein, 2023).

Disparities

Despite this progress, disparities persist, particularly concerning racial/ethnic and geographic differences in teen birth rates, as highlighted by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC, 2021). Addressing these discrepancies is a priority for local public health authorities, who aim to launch a targeted health campaign aimed at reducing teen pregnancies across diverse racial and ethnic groups.

Recent data underscores the urgency of this initiative, revealing significant gaps among different racial and ethnic populations. For instance, birth rates among Hispanic teens (25.3) and non-Hispanic Black teens (25.8) were more than twice as high as those among non-Hispanic White teens (11.4). Alarmingly, the birth rate among American Indian/Alaska Native teens (29.2) surpassed all other racial/ethnic groups (CDC, 2021).

Aim of the dataset

In the context of this complex issue the dataset collection focuses on contraceptive usage among teenagers belonging to diverse racial and ethnic backgrounds within the age group of 14-20 years. The aim of the dataset is to evaluate the effect of the previously used interventions by public health authorities as the numbers of the teenage pregnancies differ between 2014-2016. Health strategies used previously can be evaluated, improved or the new designed and implemented.

Comparison of Data between 2014 and 2016

The visualization compares the rate of the contraceptive use in the USA between 2014 and 2016. It shows that the usage of the contraceptives by teenagers aged 14-20 has risen. Thus, in some States the health strategies were successful. Public health authorities can also see in which states needs to be more done regarding interventions about teenage pregnancies. The next step would be to distinguish between diverse racial and ethnic backgrounds to investigate which intervention is more effective in which group.
